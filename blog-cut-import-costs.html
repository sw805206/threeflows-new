Four Ways to Cut Your Import Cost in a Tariff War

Four legitimate, widely underused mechanisms can dramatically reduce what you pay to bring goods into the US. Most growing brands miss most of them.

April 29, 2025

If you're importing goods from China into the US right now, you're likely paying 145% import tariffs. That's not a typo. Under the current trade environment, that number is real — and for many brands, it's turning a profitable product into an unprofitable one.

What most importers don't realize is that compliant, legitimate mechanisms exist to significantly reduce that burden. None of them is a loophole. All require proper setup and documentation. Below are four, arranged deliberately: from least hassle to most cost control. That's also, in general, the order you'll adopt them as your business grows — start simple, add sophistication as your volume justifies it.

Mechanism 1: Landed Cost — the least hassle

If you're just getting started, the mechanisms further down this list may be more complexity than you need. There's a simpler path: ask your supplier to quote you a landed cost. Have them handle global forwarding and import duty as part of the price.

You give up some optimization — the supplier prices in their margin for taking on the work — but you get predictability and zero customs headaches. When you're small, that's usually the better trade: one number, one throat to choke, and your attention stays on selling.

Mechanism 2: First Sale Valuation — requires an experienced customs broker

US Customs and Border Protection (CBP) allows importers to declare the first sale price of goods as the basis for import duty calculation — rather than the price paid by the importer to the exporter (the "last sale").

In practical terms: if your factory sells goods to a middleman at $10/unit, and the middleman sells them to your US entity at $35/unit, you may be able to declare the $10 first sale value for tariff purposes — not the $35. On a 145% tariff rate, that difference is enormous.

This is one of the more controllable levers — the setup happens on your side of the ocean. But it comes with a caveat: first sale is under US government scrutiny, and CBP expects it done right. It requires:

  • A documented, arm's-length transaction at origin
  • Evidence that the first sale price represents the true commercial value of the goods
  • Factory cooperation in providing supporting documentation (invoices, cost breakdowns)
  • Proper setup before the first shipment — you cannot retroactively apply first sale to completed imports

The way to do this correctly is to work with a legitimate, experienced customs broker who has run first sale programs before. Don't improvise it. (We can connect you with vetted brokers.)

Mechanism 3: The Chinese Export Rebate — requires negotiation skills

China's government offers export rebates — essentially a refund of a portion of the value-added tax (VAT) embedded in manufactured goods — to incentivize exports. Depending on the product category, this rebate can reach up to 13% of the declared export value.

Here's what you need to understand before you get excited: claiming the rebate is complex, and it sits under active Chinese government scrutiny. The claim must be made by a registered Chinese entity with proper VAT filing status, supported by accurate documentation at origin. This is not a process you, as a US buyer, should try to run yourself.

So why does it matter to you? Negotiation leverage. Your supplier can claim this rebate — and many quietly do. Knowing the rebate exists, and roughly what it's worth for your product category, changes the conversation. During price negotiation, ask your supplier to bake the rebate into your price. A supplier recovering up to 13% of declared value has room to move that most buyers never ask about. This works especially well if you have a stake in the game with the Chinese manufacturer — a joint venture, an equity position, or a committed long-term volume relationship gives you both the visibility and the standing to make the rebate part of the deal.

Mechanism 4: Assembly in the USA — the most cost control

At scale, the biggest lever is changing what you import in the first place. By shifting final assembly stateside, you import components rather than finished goods — which can change the tariff classification, the declared value, and the duty you pay. And the benefits stack beyond cost:

  • Cost control — duty on components instead of finished goods, and a cost structure you own end to end
  • Quality control — the final, most visible step of production happens under your eyes, not an ocean away
  • Flexibility — with assembly stateside, you can weather tariff-war uncertainties by adjusting what you import and when, rather than being locked into finished-goods shipments
  • Local incentives — state and local governments often offer incentives for assembly and manufacturing operations; depending on where you set up, that can meaningfully offset the investment

This is the most involved of the four: it takes planning, a US assembly setup, and real volume to justify. But when the economics work, they work at the level of your whole cost structure, not a single line item.

Why Most Brands/Retailers Miss This

The two most common reasons:

  1. They don't know these mechanisms exist. You can't negotiate for a rebate you've never heard of, and you can't ask about first sale if you don't know it's an option. The importers capturing these savings aren't smarter — they're better informed.
  2. They assume it's big-importer territory. First sale, rebate-aware negotiation, US assembly — they sound like Fortune 500 tools, so smaller brands never engage a broker or raise the topic with suppliers. In reality, the mechanisms scale down further than most people think.

Which One Is Right for You?

In general, the progression follows your growth: landed cost when you're just starting, first sale once volume justifies a broker relationship, rebate-aware negotiation as your supplier relationships mature, and US assembly at scale. But every product and every supply chain is different — the right sequence for you depends on your margins, your volumes, and your suppliers.
